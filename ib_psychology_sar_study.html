IB Psychology SAR Study Guide

Paper One SAR Overview

Objective: Answer Short Answer Questions (SAQs) effectively using a structured format.

Structure:

  • Introduce the topic
  • Define key terms and state your central argument
  • Present at least one study: aim, method, and results
  • Explain how the study supports your argument
  • Use accurate terminology, avoid words like "prove"
  • At least 250 words

Command Terms

  • Outline: Brief, general understanding
  • Describe: Detailed information about a topic
  • Explain (recommended): Show why something works, cause-and-effect

Short Answer Response Checklist

  • Planned answer beforehand
  • Intro includes argument and study name
  • Defined key terms
  • Used key terms accurately
  • Described study methods/results
  • Explained how study supports argument
  • Used topic sentences and qualifiers
  • No use of "prove"
  • Over 250 words

Biological Approach SAR

Question: Using one or more examples, explain localization of function.

  • Central Argument: Localization of function means specific brain areas are responsible for specific behaviors. The amygdala is linked to fear. Damage to it reduces fear.
  • Study: Feinstein et al (2011) – “SM” had damaged amygdalas and showed no fear when exposed to fear-inducing stimuli.
  • Conclusion: Supports that the amygdala is necessary for fear response, showing localization of function.

Cognitive Approach SAR

Question: Describe how one study demonstrates reconstructive memory.

  • Central Argument: Reconstructive memory means memories are not exact and can be influenced. Loftus and Palmer (1974) studied this.
  • Study: Participants watched car crashes. Verbs like “smashed” or “hit” changed their memory of the event, including false memories of broken glass.
  • Conclusion: Study shows memory is reconstructive since wording influenced what participants remembered.

Sociocultural Approach SAR

Question: Describe the social identity theory (SIT).

  • Central Argument: Social identity theory explains how people identify with in-groups and discriminate against out-groups, even when groups are meaningless.
  • Study: Tajfel et al (1970) – Boys randomly placed into groups showed favoritism for in-group members.
  • Conclusion: Supports SIT by showing that even arbitrary groups can lead to bias, proving that group identity influences behavior.
